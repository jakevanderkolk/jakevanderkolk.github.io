Course Description

This course builds on your language skills from German 101-201, especially your reading and interpretative skills, as well as your knowledge of 20th Century German culture. We will read and discuss four German youth novels set in four different historical periods from the 1920s to today:

  1. Erich Kästner's famous Emil und die Detektive takes place in the cosmopolitan Berlin of the 1920s, a time of urbanization and great cultural change.
  2. The propagandistic Trommlerbub' unterm Hakenkreuz demonstrates how the Nazis packaged their ideology in order to appeal to young people.
  3. Die Flaschenpost centers around a friendship between a boy and a girl on opposite sides of the Berlin Wall during the Cold War.
  4. Ich fühl mich so fifty-fifty follows the experiences of a girl who escapes from East Germany just months before the Wall falls.

Class discussions of the novels will be conducted in German, and you will use daily entries in a reading journal (das Lesejournal—see below) to prepare for our in-class work. You will also refine your writing skills in two formal essays. The novels will expose you to hundreds of new vocabulary words, and with systematic vocabulary practice, will expand your own lexicon. You will also watch and discuss films related to the novels.

This course requires a lot of time and energy, but offers you the possibility to significantly improve your language skills and understanding of German culture and history.

Course Assignments
  1. Participation, Preparation, Cooperation (die Teilnahme, die Vorbereitung und die Zusammenarbeit) 20%

    I will grade you on your performance in and preparing for class. I will take into consideration the following criteria:

    • Participation. You should attend and involve yourself actively in all class meetings. You should make a good-faith effort to express yourself accurately and fluently in German.
    • Preparation. You should come to class ready and willing to discuss the topics at hand.
    • Cooperation. You should support and encourage your fellow students in their efforts to use the German language and to critically engage with our target topics.
  2. Vocabulary Quizzes and Grammar Exercises (Wortschatzlernkontrollen und Grammatikübungen) 20%

    As we progress, we will also review grammar and vocabulary related to our readings.

    1. Vocabulary Quizzes. Based on the vocabulary you note in your Lesejournal Exposés, we will periodically have brief quizzes testing our ability to use (and not just recognize) new vocabulary.
    2. Grammar Exercises. We will review specific grammatical topics in Handbuch zur deutschen Grammatik as we come across them in our readings and writing. You will be graded on whether you made a good-faith effort to comprehend and complete designated exercises from this book.
  3. Reading Journal (das Lesejournal) 25%

    You will write a Lesejournal responding to this course's readings and class discussions. There are two types of entries:

    1. Reading Response (das Exposé). Prior to each class day, you will write a response to the upcoming class's reading. It should include a short summary of the text, make note key vocabulary words and phrases important for the text, and set up two critical questions for class discussion, i.e. questions that have no obvious or simply factual answer.
      • Format. The course binder Google Docs provides a model format for this assignment. Based on this format, submit your entries via Google Docs by at least two hours before class.
    2. Discussion Response (das Protokoll). During class, students will share their summaries and pool their questions, which will form the basis of our class discussion. The class will then choose a question or two to which to respond in their journals in class.
      • Format. For this in-class assignment, you may either write it via a laptop on Google Docs or by hand. Submit it by the beginning of the following class period.
    • Grading. While I may provide grammatical and lexical tips, I do not grade these assignments according to mechanics; rather, I give credit based on the thoughtfulness and effort exhibited by each entry.
  4. Essays (die Aufsätze) 2 @ 12.5% each, 25% total

    You will write two essays during the course, each focusing on a specific text.

    • Drafts. For each essay, you will submit a complete first draft. I will give you detailed feedback relating to content, stylistics, and mechanics, which you should fix for your final draft.
    • Format. You will submit your essays via Google Docs.
    • Grading. Both the first and final drafts will be graded on content, stylistics, and mechanics, with the final draft taking into consideration the improvement from the first draft.
  5. Final Project (das Abschlussprojekt) 10%

    Instead of a final exam, you will complete a creative project as the capstone assignment to this course. Feel free to propose your own project, or attempt one of the following (with my prior approval):

    1. Write and perform a dialogue for a scene that does not appear in one of the books, or which takes place before or after plot of the book.
    2. Write and perform a talk-show-style interview with a character from one of the books.
    3. Create illustrations to accompany one of the books.
    4. Create a board game based on one of the books.
    5. Write and perform a song based on one of the books.
    6. Create a website for one of the books, including information about the author, historical background, etc.
    7. Write and deliver a PowerPoint presentation on a topic related to one of the books, such as the Hitler Youth, the division of Germany after World War II, life in East Germany, etc.
    • Cooperation. You may solicit other students to help you perform your project, but each student must complete an individual project.
    • Grading. I will grade the projects on content, use of language (vocabulary and accuracy), creativity, and overall effort.

Attendance and Late Work

Your success in German 204 depends on regular attendance and active class participation.

  • Prior Notice. Please contact me in advance if you must miss class because of sanctioned college activities, illness (which requires you to remain in bed) or family emergencies. Only these reasons count as excused absences, not doctor’s appointments or travel plans.
  • Absence Penalty. Each unexcused absence beyond two will lower your final grade by 1%.
  • Number of Sessions. This class meets four times a week (two per day), so missing one whole day of class counts as two absences.
  • Late work is not accepted. Turn in your Aufsätze and Lesejournal entries on or before the due dates listed below, even if you are absent. I will accept them via Google Docs or, with handwritten Discussion Responses, in my mailbox.
Academic Integrity

College-wide. This course operates under the Kalamazoo College Honor System. That means that we treat each other with reespect, we nurture independent thought, we take responsibility for personal behavior, and we accept environmental responsibility. Academic honesty is a critical part of our value system at K. When you borrow an idea, either express it in your own words, thus thinking it through and making it your own, and acknowledge the borrowing in a note, or, in certain situations, use the exact words of the source in quotation marks and acknowledge with a note. Ideas raised in class are public domain and need not be acknowledged. If you are evern in doubt about this, you must ask.

In Foreign Language Courses. In sum, academic honesty means giving evidence to what you have learned. In the foreign language learning context, that also means the following:

  • Do not ask a TA or native speaker to proofread or correct your work. Developing your own proofreading skills is a vital part of this course.
  • Do not use translation services (online or otherwise). Especially at this level, you can probably do a much better job than an online translator.
  • Unless you are participating in a sanctioned group project, every part of what you turn in must be produced by you and you alone.

Violations. All violations of academic integrity will be investigated and determined by the course instructor, after which the case may be referred to the Dean of Students for possible disciplinary action. The first such violation will result in a grade of F for the assignment. The second violation in a given course will result in a grade of F for the course and possible suspension.

Feedback

While most of what we do is proscribed by the German Department's curriculum, there is a certain amount of leeway we have in what and how we learn for and in this course. During the term, I will have you all take a short, anonymous survey and then discuss with you the results and possible changes to the course. Likewise, please feel free to make suggestions. I cannot guarantee that your suggestion can or should be implimented, but I'm always at least interested in knowing what works or doesn't work for students.

Tips for Success

The most successful students typically do the following:

  1. Complete all of the assignments on time. A lot of what we learn and do in this course builds on what we have learned and done earlier. Missing a Exposé won't just cost you points, but will leave you unprepared for class and even your essay.
  2. Put in enough time. Language learning takes time, which is why they typically count for more credits. We expect you to spend 2 hours a day on this course, not because language learning is particularly more difficult to master, but because the work of it takes a lot of time (you have to do a lot of repetitive work, practice, and memorization). However:
  3. Learn smarter, not longer. People tend to retain knowledge better when they learn teaspoons of it at a time, and revisit those tidbits repeatedly. To really grasp something, you need time for your brain to reboot. If you tend to do all of your coursework in one or two sittings, you may not be giving yourself time to let things "sink in". Instead, do a little bit every day, give yourself time to review your work, and quite frankly time for your brain to rest.
  4. Get help when you need it. Find a tutor or ask to have a meeting with me at the point when you don't understand something, not later. Working out what you don't understand immediately will save you aggravation and extra work further down the line. Remember that we also have a lot of extra materials that you can consult if the first explanation doesn't make things clear to you.
  5. Find a learning partner. See if there is another student in this course with whom you can collaborate (not copy off of) on day-to-day assignments like the grammar exercises or readings. Find a partner one who can help fill in those gaps in your knowledge and you his/hers likewise. Collaborating with a partner, even if you don't necessarily need the help, allows you to learn different approaches to common problems.
    • However, make sure you still do your own work! You and you alone should write the assignments on which you are graded for content or correctness, like the Lesejournal and Abschlussprojekt. Collaboration does not mean dividing up questions or tasks, but rather helping each other be able to do all tasks on your own. What you turn in should be an accurate representation of what you can do on your own.
  6. Master the basics. Understanding more fundamental things is necessary for understanding more complex things. You can't learn nuclear physics without understanding what an atom is. Likewise, it gets harder to get adjective endings right if you can't put nouns in their proper cases and genders.
  7. Learn your way. There is no right way or wrong way to learn a foreign language, so find a way that works best for you. Math majors love learning grammar; they figure out language by seeing it as a system that they can manipulate like calculus. Others learn better by repetitively using the language and later coming to understand the grammatical rules in retrospect. If one way doesn't work for you, find another way. Experiment.
