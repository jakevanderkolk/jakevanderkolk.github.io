Course Description and Objectives

Prerequisite: GERM 1001. In GERM 1002, you will expand your basic language skills and learn more about the history, culture, and everyday life of German-speaking countries. These experiences will contribute to discovering your own culture and language and help us make cross-cultural comparisons.

German is spoken by people not only in Germany, Austria, Switzerland, and Liechtenstein, but also in parts of Italy, Belgium, the Netherlands, France, Eastern Europe, the U.S., and Latin America. A good pronunciation of German is an asset for voice majors and knowing German will help you understand the works and ideas of musicians, philosophers, scientists, and writers who contributed greatly to forming our world. Knowing more about the cultures of German-speaking countries helps understand and even question assumptions we make about our own society.

In this course you will build on the cultural knowledge and communicative skills of speaking, listening, reading, and writing that you acquired German 1001. After completing GERM 1002, your linguistic proficiency should be displaying novice-mid or even novice-high proficiency on the ACTFL scale or A1 on the Common European Framework of References for Languages scale.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, students will be able to
  1. communicate and exchange information on familiar topics — including everyday activities, ordering food at a restaurant, shopping for groceries and clothing, and playing various sports — using phrases and simple sentences, sometimes supported by memorized language
  2. handle short social interactions in everyday situations by asking and answering simple questions
  3. write short messages and notes on familiar topics related to everyday life
  4. recognize pieces of information, words, phrases, and simple sentences related to everyday life in spoken German and sometimes understand the main topic of what is being said
  5. understand familiar words, phrases, and sentences in short and simple texts related to everyday life
  6. sometimes understand the main idea of a more difficult text you have read

Homework (die Hausaufgaben, 25%)

Each day you should check the homework plan posted on Canvas. It is your responsibility to complete homework as assigned on a daily basis.

Expect to spend up to 2 hours on homework and preparation for each class session. Please see me if you feel you are working more than this amount.

There is no partial credit for individual assignments. To get full credit, you must do all of the following:
  • On Time. You submit the set of assignments on time, even if you will be absent that day.
  • Completion. You submit all parts of all assignments due that day. Every single blank of every single assignment must be filled in.
  • Present a good faith effort, that is, you try to genuinely engage with the material. It is not good faith to just copy-and-paste answers from the answer sheet or give a one word answer for a question asking for a complete sentence.
  • Correction. You reattempt exercises at least once if you find significant problems.

There are three different types of homework assignments:

  1. "Lernen" Homework. Online exercises done before class.
  2. "Zeigen" Homework. Online exercises done shortly after a class.
  3. Quizlet Vocabulary. Each session will require you to complete "Flashcards", "Learn", and "Test" activities from Quizlet.

Quizzes (die Lernkontrollen, 22%)

Each week will have a quiz on vocabulary and grammar. Each quiz assumes continued mastery of previous topics.

  • Missing a Quiz. Each quiz must be taken by posted test date, not later. If you will miss a quiz session, make plans with me to take it before the session.

Projects (die Projekte, 15%, 5% each)

You will complete three group projects designed to showcase your communicative skills in German.

Final Assessment (die Endprüfung, 10%)

You will complete a final assessment during exam week, which will gauge your reading, writing, listening, and speaking skills along with your cultural knowledge.

Cultural Engagement (das kulturelle Engagement, 3%)

Over the semester, you will participate in at least three cultural engagement activities outside of class related to German-speaking culture, like participating a film night, attending a talk by a speaker, or attending a German Club chat hour. Elligible events will be announced as the semester goes on. You will submit a report for each of the three events through Canvas.

How to Succeed

The most successful students typically do the following:

  • Exercise Self Care
    • Get your sleep! 7-9 hours (in a row).
    • Eat well and exercise. Manage your diet and get some physical activity every day.
    • Be mindful of substance use. A clear brain works and feels better.
    • Get involved. Make friends. Participate in sports, clubs, etc. Find your niche.
  • Get your money's worth:
    • Use office hours. Email me to make an appointment, or feel free to "stop by" with questions! Don't suffer from FMOOWMP! →
    • Talk a lot! Make lots of mistakes! Talking a lot instead of well is the best way to learn. You will make mistakes as you try out new vocabulary and grammar. This shows me how you've been learning.
    • Ask questions! When you’re lost, confused, or have a question, someone else probably has the same one (and they’ll be glad you asked).
    • Overwhelmed? Frustrated? Get help NOW! This course builds on itself, so any issue you have now will only get bigger. Save yourself aggravation and extra work further down the line by taking advantage of the extra videos I post, e-mailing me, coming to my office hours, or using our free German tutors through the Pirate Academic Success Center. Book an appointment via tutoring@ecu.edu or 252-737-3009.
  • Keep up. College language classes move fast and require sustained engagement. Students most often fail language courses because they fall behind. Make sure to:
    • Show up. Even with make-up assignments, missing one or (even worse) several sessions in a row makes everything you learn afterwards harder. Make things easier on yourself and be here when you can. Be absent as sparingly as possible!
    • Complete all assignments on time. Missing a day's worth of homework won't just cost you points, it will leave you unprepared for the next day's lesson and the next assignment.
  • Learn smarter, not longer. Studies show that people retain knowledge better and quicker when they learn small amounts at a time and review repeatedly. With a little foresight and planning, you can save time and frustration if you:
    • Build a routine. Set aside a time every day, every other day, or every few hours to do your work. Make it a habit.
    • Small tasks. Cramming is much less efficient than breaking assignments into a series of smaller, manageable tasks that allow your mind time to digest what it just learned.
    • Be opportunistic. Use time that you might waste (e.g. standing in a long line, eating by yourself, waiting for class to start) practicing vocabulary instead of viewing cat photos on your phone.
    • Take breaks. Give yourself time to let things sink in when studying. Schedule a break every so often so that you don't work for more than an hour at a time.
    • If you get stuck, revisit later. If a particular lesson or exercise frustrates you, put it aside for a while, work on something else for a while, and let your brain reset. It may make sense later.
    • Put in the time. Language learning isn't hard, but it does take time. We expect you to work up to 6 hours a week outside of class on coursework.
    • If you need more than 6 hours/week to keep up, or just feel overwhelmed, get help! Come and see me in my office hours, so that we can hash out methods that will work specifically for you.
  • Find study partners. Get to know your classmates and form groups in which you can collaborate on day-to-day assignments, and study for tests. But make sure you still do your own work!
  • Master the basics. We learn the most basic language first because it is the most important, so be patient. Before you can sprint, you need to know how to crawl.
    • Put effort into your homework. Homework teaches you the basics, which we will then practice in more sophisticated ways in class. Putting a genuine effort into homework is vital to your success.
    • Vocabulary, vocabulary, vocabulary! Having a strong working vocabulary makes everything easier and faster. Learn a few words every day. Use our Quizlet utilities or (even better) old-fashioned paper flash cards – find what works for you!
  • Make learning fun! Make class fun and welcoming for yourself and your classmates by participating in lighthearted yet respectful ways.
  • Celebrate your progress! You are doing something amazing: Over the next 15 weeks you will learn what took you years in your first language. Celebrate that every once in a while.

Academic Integrity

Aside from university policy, note the following issues specific to our class:
Acceptable. DO THIS! Unacceptable. DON'T DO THIS!
Adapt an expression you saw in Impuls Deutsch for your own work. Copy out whole sentences from another source and claim it as your own work.
Ask someone to underline, but not correct, the mistakes in your essay. Have a native speaker, friend, peer, or your mom provide corrections to your essay (effectively re-writing it for you).
Look up vocabulary words in Impuls Deutsch or Quizlet. Use an online translator to translate a sentence or paragraph.*
Paraphrase an outside source and use a citation. Use outside sources without citing.
Use Impuls Deutsch and your notes to check your genders, spellings, and plural forms. Use a grammar checker to identify and revise your mistakes.
Use the expressions and grammar you have learned the best you can on a project. Use on a project grammar and vocabulary that we haven't learned (I will assume you got them from illicit sources).
*Online translators haven't been the best at translating. A student, who was going to be absent in a course, once sent this email to a colleague named Rob:
Lieber Fruchtsirup: Ich verpasse heute die Kategorie.

English: Dear Fruit Syrup: I'm passing by the category today.†
†She had entered "Dear Rob: I'm missing class today." in an online translator.
Instead, rely first on the vocabulary you have learned and thus know well!
