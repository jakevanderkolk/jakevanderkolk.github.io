The Challenge: Translation software has become too good. New developments in artificial intelligence, augmented reality, and mobile computing have made possible language tools and apps that present new challenges to foreign language instruction. The convenience and enhanced accuracy of deep-learning online translators, automated proofreaders, and real-time electronic interpreters ← make their abuse more appealing to language learners and more difficult to detect for language instructors. Traditional plagiarism countermeasures that rely on forewarning and enforcing negative sanctions have difficulty in dissuading these practices.

Preventing dishonesty without banning technology. To combat the abuse of these technologies, we draw on nudge theory to suggest strategies that proactively nudge students away from bad practices without curtailing the availability of technology in the classroom or compromising known best practices by
  1. Creating a classroom culture of empathetic accountability,
  2. Preempting student attitudes and habits that can lead to dishonesty, and
  3. Adjusting student perceptions in favor of honest practices.

Potentional Solutions

This presentation develops interventions inspired by nudge theory as outlined by the United Kingdom goverment's Behavioural Insights Team. Rather than influencing behavior only through the prospect and enforcement of negative consequences, nudge theory aims to a modify a person's or group's "choice architecture", or mental calculus of self interest, through subtle incentives and indirect suggestions, e.g. by putting fruit at eye level rather than banning junk food. To implement such strategies, the Behaviorial Insights Team suggests the following so-called "EAST" tactics:

  • Make it Easy. If possible, the desired choice should offer the path of least resistance, e.g. by making it the default option, by reducing hassles associated with that option, or by simplifying the messaging pertaining to such a choice. (Behavioural Insights Team 4)
  • Make it Attractive. The desired choice should be aesthetically pleasing. Reinforce behaviors by using materials that draw attention, or design rewards and sanctions that make the desired option desireable beyond its immediate economic value (i.e. its grade value). (Behavioural Insights Team 5)
  • Make it Social. The desired choice should be viewed as the socially preferred option. Show that most students behave in the target manner, use existing social networks to normalize desired habits, and encourage accountability among a class. (Behavioural Insights Team 4)
  • Make it Timely. Target interventions for times when they will be most effective. Prompt students when they are most receptive, make desired choices appear more immediately appealing in the short, and provide scaffolding that helps students make the desired choice later on. (Behavioural Insights Team 6)

Foster classroom accountability and empathy

By building a sense of accountability and empathy in the classroom, teachers can dissuade dishonest behaviors. By letting oneself be seen as an "actual human being" and by encouraging communality among among students, the instructor can induce a sense of mutual obligation in students that adds emotional consequences to dishonesty.

Make yourself personable.

Reduce the sense of stratification between teacher and student by developing a personal repore with students. Actively let your students know that you care about them, their best interests, and their personal development.

  • Have individual getting-to-know-you meetings. At the beginning of each term, have each student fill out a survey about themselves and then come to your office hours for a 5 minute meeting just to get to know each other (but plan on meetings lasting a little longer because you will find your students interesting). Not only will it help your student find your office and become comfortable in such a one-on-one setting, but it will help them see you as approachable and a real person with feelings and sympathies. Don't underestimate the Walmart Greeter Effect, that is, how being acknowledged by another human being can make one more conscientious of social obligations and aware that they are not anonymous and free of observation.

Glorify positive behaviors.

Rather than only demonizing bad practices, make academically honest practices attractive to students by exhaulting their benefits beyond just getting a good grade.

  • Compare negative and positive examples. Alongside your plagiarism statement include a list of desirable practices juxtaposed to bad practices, which not only helps students understand the distinctions between these practices, but also provides alternatives that they can use instead. Then have students take an open-book quiz that tests knowledge of these distinctions (along with other basics of the course) which they can retake an infinite number of times. Not only are students forced to learn what counts as academic dishonesty, instructors also gain documentation that students (should) know appropriate behavior.
  • Model successful study habits. Provide a list of effective study practices, i.e. "Tips for Success", and emphasize how most past successful students used these tactics. Not only does this simply how students can engage with the coursework, it applies a bit of "peer pressure" and a sense of obligation in the same way as the Behavioural Insights Team increased tax payment rates by advertising that most citizens paid their taxes. Likewise note the utility of these habits beyond this particular course, e.g. how a particular memorization technique can be used in other classes.
  • Give acclaim and other small rewards for honest performances. Providing a small but meaningful acknowledgement (e.g. with a "collectible" tchotchke) for honestly-earned (but not necessarily perfect) writing reinforces those positive efforts. By exhibiting and praising a particular honest work (without reference to its originator if needed) can incentivize other students to act accordingly.
  • Group students strategically. Isolate individual students with higher chances of academically dishonest behavior from similar students and place or team them with students who exhibit positive academic and study habits. The at-risk student will not only witness that academically honest study habits are common among classmates, but this student may "apprentice" such skills from classmates.

Preempt Risky Attitudes

Instructors can mitigate feelings of frustration and desperation before they lead to plagiarism by cultivating support structures, such as study groups, accessible feedback, and supplementary materials, and by proactively intervening with at-risk students.

Change Perceptions

Through careful instructional design, instructors can have students implicitly change their mental calculus toward plagiarism by making honest methods easier, more advantageous to the student's short and long-term goals, and more enjoyable than dishonest methods.
